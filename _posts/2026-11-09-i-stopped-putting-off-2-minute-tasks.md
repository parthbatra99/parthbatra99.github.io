I read a book called *Million Dollar Weekend* by Noah Kagan. Has it made me a millionaire? No. But it did something more useful: it gave me a rule so stupidly simple that I'm annoyed I didn't figure it out myself. If a task takes less than two minutes, I don't write it down, I don't add it to my notes, I don't schedule it. I just do it.

That's it. That's the whole rule. And somehow it bumped my daily output by roughly 40%.

The book isn't really about making a million dollars. It's about how to get things done, period. The core idea is something Noah calls the **action bias effect**. Think of it like going to the gym. You train your muscles to lift weights, and after a while those muscles can't tell the difference between a barbell and your couch. They just lift. Your brain works the same way. Train it to act on things immediately and it starts doing it everywhere, not just in the scenarios where you consciously decided to be productive.

## How it actually works in practice

My professor emails me asking for some data I already have. Old me would have read the email, thought "I'll get to that later," added it to my to-do list, and then spent more mental energy remembering to do it than actually doing it. The task takes five minutes. Maybe three if I stop overthinking the greeting line.

Now I just reply. Right there. Done.

Same thing with dishes after a meal. I used to leave them in the sink like some kind of archaeological exhibit, a layered record of everything I ate that week. Washing them takes two minutes. Two. I was spending more time walking past them and feeling guilty about them than it would take to just wash the damn things.

This sounds trivial because it is trivial. But stacking up 15 to 20 trivial tasks a day that you actually complete instead of deferring? That compounds fast.

## The bookstore incident

Noah has another exercise in the book: whenever you buy something, ask for a 10% discount. I don't drink coffee, so I couldn't do the classic "ask the barista" version. But I bought the book itself at a bookstore in Zurich, so a few weeks later I went back for round two.

I grabbed a book and some pens, walked to the counter, and asked for a 10% discount. The person behind the counter looked at me like I'd asked to borrow their car. "Do you have the membership card?" No. "Are you a student?" Yes. "Do you have the student membership card?" Also no. Then they just stared into my soul. "Then why should you get a discount?"

I mumbled something about just asking. Paid full price. Walked out.

The whole metro ride home I was replaying the conversation in my head and laughing. Total failure by every measurable standard. But here's what actually happened: my ability to ask for things got noticeably better after that. I asked a friend for a favor, didn't work out. Asked my professor about an opportunity, and that one did work out. The muscle got trained through a failed rep.

If you never ask, the answer is always no. The person on the other end literally cannot read your mind. You have to say the thing.

## Your dream life costs less than you think

This was the part that surprised me. The book makes you sit down with a pen and paper and actually calculate what your dream life costs. Not the vague "I want to be rich" version. The specific version. The vacations, the car, the classes, the whatever.

Turns out you don't need a million dollars. You need maybe $2,000 to $5,000 extra a month. That's it. That covers the trips, the hobbies, the lifestyle. The million-dollar number is a story we tell ourselves because we've never bothered to do the actual math. Once you write it down and put real prices next to real things, the number shrinks to something that feels, if not easy, then at least possible.

I'd recommend doing this exercise even if you're happy where you are. It clarifies what you actually want versus what you think you should want.

## Start from where you are

Here's a mistake I used to make constantly. I'd want to start something, a project, a channel, a business idea, and I'd immediately look for the perfect entry point somewhere far away. Some connection I didn't have. Some market I wasn't in.

Noah's advice: start from your circle of influence. Your friends, your family, your colleagues, the people at your language class or your sports club. That's your starting radius. You expand from there.

This YouTube channel started exactly that way. I didn't launch it by cold-emailing creators in the US or Japan. I started writing, showed early drafts to my friends, asked if the point landed, if the framing made sense. Slow feedback loop. Gradual improvement. The circle expands on its own once the work gets better.

And it's not just me. The book is full of examples. Someone started a grass-fed bone broth business from their network. Another person made prism glasses for rock climbers so they wouldn't strain their necks looking up for five hours straight. My personal favorite: a service where you pay someone to stand in line for you when the next iPhone drops. That person was making $80,000 a year. To stand in lines. Every opportunity I've had, scholarships, positions, jobs, has come through my circle of influence. Not a single one came from cold outreach to a stranger on the internet.

## Give yourself 48 hours

The title says it all. Million Dollar *Weekend*. As in: 48 hours. That's the time bound Noah suggests you give yourself to start something.

When I started sharing my writing, I told myself I'd produce the first draft by the end of that weekend. Done. When I wanted to write something, same deal: this weekend, finished. A friend needed advice on speaking clearly, so I sat down, recorded an entire audio lesson, and sent it over. One weekend.

The trick isn't that weekends are magical. The trick is that a deadline of "someday" is the same as "never." Give yourself two days and your brain stops negotiating. At some point you stop distinguishing weekends from weekdays entirely, and what's left is just the habit of trying things.

You get 52 weekends a year. That's 52 shots at something new. A hobby, a business idea, a side project, a language, a conversation you've been putting off. 80% of your results will come from 20% of those experiments, but you can't know which 20% without running the full set.

You don't need a grand plan. You need a two-minute task you've been avoiding and the willingness to just start it. Everything after that is just momentum wearing a convincing disguise.
