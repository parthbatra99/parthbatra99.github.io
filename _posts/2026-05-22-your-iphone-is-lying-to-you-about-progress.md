I've been reading [*Chip War*](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Chip-War/Chris-Miller/9781982172008) this month and one idea from it has been stuck in my head. We've been trained to expect linear technological progress. New iPhone every year, 20% faster, 20% more efficient. Science doesn't work that way.

A colleague recently shipped a project that took almost two years. For the first year and a half, nothing worked. Just optimization, failure, more optimization. Then devices started working and results came fast. If they were a smartphone company, they'd have to show progress every single year. How do you show progress when your devices aren't working yet?

Here's what companies actually do. Say a manufacturing breakthrough gives a 35% improvement in processor performance. They don't ship all 35% at once. They split it: 20% this year, 20% next year. The growth came in one big chunk, but they sell it in a line.

Also, those bar charts companies show at keynotes? A 35% improvement bar drawn next to the previous year's bar is sometimes three times taller, not a third taller. That's not 35%. That's 3x. Big companies are straight up manipulating data visualization and nobody calls them out.

Hardware progress has been slowing for 15 to 20 years. We're at the physical limits of how small a transistor can get. The improvements you see now are mostly software, not hardware. They're not the same thing.

Once you see the line for what it is, the yearly upgrade cycle stops feeling like progress and starts feeling like a release schedule.
