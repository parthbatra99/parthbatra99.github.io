Last month a recruiter emailed me about a role. I wasn't interested. The old me would have ghosted, or at best fired back a four-word "no thanks, good luck" and called it manners. Instead I spent three minutes writing why it wasn't a fit, what I am actually looking for, and that I'd remember them if the shape of the role changed.

Nobody asked me to do that. The recruiter didn't notice the difference, and the extra two minutes changed nothing about that conversation. I did it anyway, and the reason is the whole point of this post.

There's a class of decision I used to get wrong constantly: the last 5% of a thing. The email is 95% written, so I hit send without the proofread. The workout is 95% done, so I skip the last set. The code works, so I skip the test. The conversation is 95% listening, and I spend the other 5% quietly rehearsing what I'll say next.

The last 5% is the part nobody sees and nobody asks for. It's also the only part that matters, and not for the reason you'd think.

The naive read is that the last 5% makes the work better. It does, a little. The email is marginally more polished, the last rep is marginally more growth. But if that were the whole payoff, it wouldn't be worth it, because most of the time nobody notices and the outcome is identical.

The real payoff is upstream of the work. Every time I finish the last 5%, I cast a tiny vote. The vote says: I'm the kind of person who does things properly, even when nobody's watching, even when it doesn't matter, even when I could get away with less. And votes accumulate.

A single well-finished thing is worth almost nothing. A hundred of them is a different person. The standard isn't a dial you set per task. It's one dial, and it drifts in whichever direction you push it most often.

I noticed this first in code. Early on I'd leave `// TODO: fix later` comments and ship. Felt efficient. Six months later I'd be debugging my own ghosts at 2am, cursing a past version of myself who clearly could not be trusted. The TODOs never got fixed. They just bred.

The day I started actually closing them before the PR went up, two things happened. The code got better, sure. But the bigger thing: I stopped dreading my own old code, because I could finally trust the person who wrote it. That trust made the next commit faster, because I wasn't bolting defensive scaffolding onto work I secretly suspected was shaky.

It works the same way outside code. The thank-you note with one specific line instead of "thanks." The set where you actually hit depth instead of phoning in the rep. The conversation where you shut up and listen instead of loading your reply while the other person is still talking. None of these move the visible outcome. All of them move the next outcome, because they move the operator.

Here's the reframe that took me years to see. Doing things well is not a quality strategy. It's an identity strategy. The point of the well-written email is not a better email. The point is that you become someone who writes emails like that, and that person then writes better code, lifts better, listens better, ships better, because they're not a different person in each room. The standard travels with you.

Most people keep separate dials. Work-dial at 90, gym-dial at 40, relationships-dial at 60, dishes-dial at 20. They assume these are independent. They are not. The person who leaves dishes in the sink "because it doesn't matter" is the same person who'll ship the half-tested model "because it works on my machine," is the same person who'll glance at their phone while you're mid-sentence. The dial is one dial. You can't keep the low settings quarantined. They bleed.

---

Now, a caveat I mean sincerely. This isn't "do everything perfectly." Perfectionism is a different disease, and it usually ships nothing because nothing is ever ready. The last 5% rule is the opposite of that. It's about finishing things, properly, and moving on. Perfectionism says "it's not ready." The last 5% says "it's ready, I did it right, next."

The test is simple. Could I find one more minute to make this better, and would future-me be glad I spent it? Usually yes. Sometimes no, and then you ship. The only way to learn which is which is to default to yes until you've earned the right to say no. People who default to no are not being efficient. They're just practicing a different identity, and getting very good at it.

I write this blog "unedited on purpose," and that's half true. I don't over-polish the prose, and I post things that aren't ready by plenty of standards. But every post gets the last 5% that matters to me: the structure holds, the diagram makes sense, the claim is something I'd defend over a drink. I skip the 5% that's vanity and I never skip the 5% that's load-bearing. That line is the whole craft.

The reason I bother isn't that you'll notice. You won't, most of the time. The reason is that every time I do it, the person who writes the next post is slightly more likely to do it too. And that person is the one I have to live with for the rest of my life.

You don't do things well to improve the work. You do it because the work is the only place you get to rehearse being the kind of person you want to be, and you are, whether you admit it or not, always rehearsing.
