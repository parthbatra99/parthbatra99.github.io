Except your brain scan tells a different story. Before 11:05, before you were even consciously aware of wanting to read, the scan already predicted you'd pick up that book. Not just that you'd read something, but *which* book. Not just which book, but the exact paragraph where you'd stop. Your brain made the call before "you" did.

That's the opening of a chapter I read recently, and it messed me up for the rest of the day.

## Five Ways to Kill Someone

Then it gets worse. The chapter walks through five scenarios where a person is killed. A 5-year-old finds his dad's loaded gun and accidentally shoots a woman. A 12-year-old, abused for years, intentionally shoots someone who was teasing him. A 26-year-old who suffered childhood abuse kills his girlfriend. A 28-year-old with loving parents and zero trauma shoots a stranger for fun.

Your moral reflex kicks in hard on that fourth one. Psychopath. Monster. Lock him up and throw away the key.

But then comes scenario five. Same guy, same crime, same background. Only this time, an MRI reveals a golf ball-sized tumor pressing against his prefrontal cortex, the region that controls impulses and emotions. Suddenly the monster becomes a patient. The tumor made him do it. He couldn't help it. You feel sympathy instead of rage.

Here's the question that follows you around for the rest of the week: what exactly is the difference between a tumor you didn't choose and a childhood you didn't choose?

## The Puppet Loves the Strings

The argument isn't that actions don't have consequences. If you shoot someone, that person dies. The physics works fine. The argument is about where the *thought* of shooting came from in the first place. You didn't author that thought. It bubbled up from an accumulation of genes, environment, upbringing, random encounters, and things you absorbed without knowing it.

You're basically a puppet. The puppet has free will right up until it decides to love the strings.

I realize this sounds like late-night dorm room philosophy. But the neuroscience is real, and the implications are uncomfortable in a way that feels productive, not nihilistic. Stick with me.

## Your Success Is Mostly Not Yours

The argument makes a point that hit close to home. I was born in a village of roughly 500 households. When I was around three and a half, my parents and maternal grandparents decided to move me to a small town nearby because the village schools weren't great. That one decision, which I had absolutely nothing to do with, set off a chain of outcomes that put me where I am today.

Other kids born in the same village, the same month, the same year? Not all of them ended up studying at top colleges. Not because they were less capable, but because the initial conditions diverged. Malcolm Gladwell makes a similar observation in *Outliers*: elite Canadian hockey players are disproportionately born in the first few months of the year. Big tech CEOs cluster within a single birth year. The pattern keeps showing up.

Very little of your success is actually in your control. If that sentence makes you uncomfortable, good. It should.

## How Creativity Actually Works

But there's a flip side, and it's the part that redeemed the whole video for me. If your brain is essentially a quantum computer with millions of neurons firing at random, you can't control which neurons fire and when. You can't control the collisions. But you *can* control what goes into the system.

Picture every idea you've ever absorbed as a spherical ball floating around in your head. Every book, every conversation, every YouTube video at 2 AM. When two of those balls collide, a new idea is born. Most collisions produce ideas that someone else has already had. Ball 5 hits ball 32 in your head, and it turns out ball 72 hit ball 58 in mine, and somehow we both invented the same thing.

A genius idea is just a collision between two balls that have never collided in anyone's head before. That's it. There's no magic, no divine spark. Just unique combinations of existing inputs.

The only lever you have is choosing which balls to put in your head in the first place.

## The Real Takeaway

This is where the chapter lands its punch. Every piece of content you consume, even the garbage, even the stuff you scroll past and forget, plants a ball in your head. It stays there subconsciously. It affects which collisions happen next.

So the question isn't whether you have free will. You probably don't, at least not in the way you think. The question is whether you're curating the inputs to the only system you partially control.

I'm not sure I chose to write this post. But I am sure about which ideas I want floating around in my head tomorrow.
