> "If you can keep your head when all about you / Are losing theirs and blaming it on you…"

That's the opening of Rudyard Kipling's ["If—"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poems/46473/if---). It ends, 32 lines later, with the one everybody knows:

> "Yours is the Earth and everything that's in it, / And—which is more—you'll be a Man, my son!"

Written in 1910. Recited at graduations, framed on walls, set to cinematic music over drone footage a thousand times on YouTube. The whole thing is gorgeous. Every line lands. And every single one is *alone*.

> "If you can dream—and not make dreams your master…"

Lovely. Dream big. Just don't bother telling anyone the dream.

> "Or watch the things you gave your life to, broken, / And stoop and build 'em up with worn-out tools…"

Stoic. Beautiful. Notice the verb. *Stoop*. Alone. In the dark. With your worn-out tools. Nobody hands you a flashlight.

> "If you can fill the unforgiving minute / With sixty seconds' worth of distance run…"

There's your closing image. A man, running, by himself, the whole way. Maybe that was strength in 1910. Our generation inherited the poem verbatim. We just upgraded the wifi and forgot to import the community.

---

## The diagnosis

Read the whole thing and notice who's in the room. Your mother isn't. Your partner isn't. Your friends show up twice, once as "crowds" and once as "foes," which is roughly how most action movies treat relationships too. The poem is a portrait of a man who stands alone, conquers alone, suffers alone, and then, at the very end, turns to address a son who isn't allowed to help either.

I went deep on this poem and what it says about how we think about strength, and I haven't stopped thinking about it since. Because here's the catch. The poem isn't wrong about resilience or integrity. Those matter. It just presents a world where the ultimate achievement is self-sufficiency, and self-sufficiency, sold hard enough and long enough, turns into a beautifully furnished isolation.

The man it describes is the man I was taught to be. Probably the man you were taught to be too. And he's lonely.

---

## Nobody did it alone. Nobody.

That's not motivation-poster talk. That's history. Einstein didn't conjure special relativity from thin air. He built on [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations) and decades of work by physicists most people have never heard of. The narrative that one genius woke up one morning and rewrote physics is a story we tell because it's clean and dramatic. The reality is messier: 99 people took 99 steps, and the 100th person crossed the finish line and got the trophy.

Apple didn't happen because one man was brilliant in a garage. It happened because the U.S. military poured billions into semiconductor research during the Cold War, an entire industry bloomed in Silicon Valley, and Jobs happened to be standing in the right place at the right time with the right instincts. Smart man. Good instincts. But the ground was laid by thousands of people he'd never meet.

We keep believing the solo-legend story anyway. Because it's flattering. Because it lets each of us imagine we could be that one person. Because the internet has been pumping fuel on this fire for decades, selling the idea that true strength is needing nobody.

This is where our loneliness starts. Not with some sudden cultural collapse. With a poem, a movie, a thousand stories that all say the same thing: strong people handle it alone. We grew up on that. Now the [Survey Center on American Life](https://www.americansurveycenter.org/why-mens-social-circles-are-shrinking/) keeps finding the same thing, that 28 percent of men under 30 report having no close social connections at all, and we sit there wondering why.

## We were trained for this

Here's the part I can't stop turning over. The number isn't a glitch. It's a curriculum, and I passed it.

I grew up around emotionally unavailable men. That's not a complaint, it's just the weather. If you know any mid-20s Indian men, you know we are a nonchalant final boss. We don't say "I love you," we say "love you bro," and only under extreme duress, and only then because it's the highest compliment physically possible in our register. Asking for help is a foreign language. Needing someone is a leak you patch before anyone notices.

That's Kipling's man. Keep your head. Trust yourself. Stooping alone with the worn-out tools. We didn't invent the archetype. We just inherited it wholesale and called it culture.

I remember being nineteen and having a genuinely bad week, the kind where something inside you quietly cracks. I picked up my phone and scrolled through the contacts. Two hundred names. I could not find one I would actually call. That's not a tech problem. That's a training problem. I had spent years getting very good at being unreachable, and I had succeeded.

---

## The part I wish someone had told me earlier

If nobody did it alone, can one individual even aspire to do something extraordinary? That question bugged me. If you can't find a team, if nobody around you sees the vision, should you just give up?

No. Here's how it actually works.

One individual can always *start* the journey. Your dream is big, which means nobody around you can see what you see. That's normal. If everyone understood your vision, it probably wasn't that ambitious. So you believe in it first. You start acting on it. Your actions do two things: they solidify your own belief that this is possible, and they ignite belief in the people around you.

And here's the part I wish someone had told me earlier. You will get tired. Not halfway through. Not at 50%. You'll be exhausted by the time you've done 10% of the journey. I felt it a few months into a side project I'd started after work. It was a voice-AI thing, nothing fancy, and for three months straight nothing I built actually worked. Every evening I'd open my laptop at 9pm, break something, fix it, break it differently, close the laptop at midnight, and lie there convinced I was polishing a wrong turn. I was ready to quit. I'd already written the polite "pausing this for a bit" message in my head.

The thing that stopped me wasn't grit. It was that I'd already told two friends what I was chasing. Quitting now meant looking each of them in the eye and taking it back. That's when the belief you planted in other people carries you. Because now they're invested. Your quit feels like their quit. They won't let you stop.

It's not weakness to need that. It's architecture.

## The mirror and the expectations

There's a second trap ambitious people fall into, and it's the mirror image of the first one. Parents, siblings, that cousin who made it big and now everyone compares you to them. It piles up.

I used to see expectations as weight on my shoulders. Now I see them differently. When someone expects something from you, it means they believe you're capable of it. Nobody expects things from people they've given up on. Think about that. The last time you expected something from someone knowing full well they wouldn't deliver, how'd that feel? You don't do it. Expectations are a sign that someone still thinks you can do more.

I've made the mistake of pushing back on people who held me to standards I set myself. Friends who got angry when I fell below my own moral bar. My first reaction was defensive. "Who are you to tell me?" Classic ego move. But they weren't being unfair. They were reminding me of something I'd declared, something I believed in, and I was the one walking away from it.

Sometimes the people who love you hold up a mirror, and your first instinct is to break it. That's not their problem. That's yours. And the scarier scenario isn't people expecting too much from you. It's people expecting nothing.

---

## Maybe that was the test in 1910

Kipling wrote "you'll be a Man, my son." For his generation, standing alone when everything fell apart was probably the test. I don't think it's ours.

The real test isn't keeping your head when all about you are losing theirs. It's letting someone stand next to you when you're not sure you deserve it. Picking up the phone at midnight instead of scrolling past the contacts. Answering the "love you bro" with something back, before the person sending it learns to stop sending it.

We're still running the 1910 distance run. Sixty seconds of distance, every unforgiving minute, alone. I think the poem got the strength part right and the company part catastrophically wrong. You can keep your head. You can fill the minute. You just don't have to fill it by yourself.
