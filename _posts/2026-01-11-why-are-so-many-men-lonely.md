If you can keep your head when all about you are losing theirs, you'll be a man, my son.

That's how Rudyard Kipling's poem ["If—"](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poems/46473/if---) ends. Written in 1910, it's been recited at graduations, framed on walls, and turned into probably a thousand motivational YouTube videos with cinematic music and drone footage. It's beautiful. It's also a blueprint for loneliness.

I went deep on this poem and what it reveals about how our whole generation thinks about strength, and I haven't stopped thinking about it since.

---

The poem tells you to trust yourself when others doubt you. To wait without tiring. To dream but not make dreams your master. To build things up with worn-out tools after watching everything you built get destroyed.

Notice what's missing? Other people. Your mother isn't in there. Your partner isn't in there. Your friends show up as "crowds" and "foes," which is basically how most action movies treat relationships too. The poem is a portrait of someone who stands alone, conquers alone, and suffers alone. It's a 125-year-old idea of strength, and our generation is still living inside it, just with better wifi and worse community.

Here's the thing that struck me: the poem isn't wrong about resilience or integrity. Those matter. But it presents a world where the ultimate achievement is self-sufficiency, and that's a trap.

---

Nobody did it alone. Nobody.

That's not motivation-poster talk. That's history. Einstein didn't conjure special relativity from thin air. He built on [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations) and decades of work by physicists most people have never heard of. The narrative that one genius woke up one morning and rewrote physics is a story we tell because it's clean and dramatic. The reality is messier: 99 people took 99 steps, and the 100th person crossed the finish line and got the trophy.

I read the [Steve Jobs biography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Jobs_(book)) and then read [*Chip War*](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Chip-War/Chris-Miller/9781982172008) shortly after. Put those two books together and you realize that Apple didn't happen because one man was brilliant in a garage. It happened because the U.S. military poured billions into semiconductor research during the Cold War, an entire industry bloomed in Silicon Valley, and Jobs happened to be standing in the right place at the right time with the right instincts. He was smart. He had good ideas. But the ground was laid by thousands of people he'd never meet.

Arnold Schwarzenegger [once said something similar](https://www.marketplace.org/story/2017/05/12/schwarzenegger-self-made-man-myth): "I didn't do it alone. It just looks like that."

---

So why do we keep believing the solo-legend story? Because it's flattering. Because it lets each of us imagine that we could be that one person. And because the internet has been pumping fuel on this fire for decades, selling the idea that true strength is needing nobody.

This is where our loneliness starts. Not with some sudden cultural collapse. With a poem, a movie, a thousand stories that all say the same thing: strong people handle it alone. We grew up on that. Now surveys keep finding the same thing, that roughly one in four people under 30 say they have no one to call at 2am, and we sit there wondering why.

---

If nobody did it alone, can one individual even aspire to do something extraordinary? That question bugged me. If you can't find a team, if nobody around you sees the vision, should you just give up?

No. Here's how it actually works.

One individual can always *start* the journey. Your dream is big, which means nobody around you can see what you see. That's normal. If everyone understood your vision, it probably wasn't that ambitious. So you believe in it first. You start acting on it. Your actions do two things: they solidify your own belief that this is possible, and they ignite belief in the people around you.

And here's the part I wish someone had told me earlier. You will get tired. Not halfway through. Not at 50%. You'll be exhausted by the time you've done 10% of the journey. I felt it a few months into a side project I'd started after work, when nothing I built was working and I was ready to call it a wrong turn. The thing that kept me going wasn't grit. It was that I'd already told two friends what I was chasing, and quitting now meant looking each of them in the eye and taking it back. That's when the belief you planted in other people carries you. Because now they're invested. Your quit feels like their quit. They won't let you stop.

It's not weakness to need that. It's architecture.

---

There's a second trap ambitious people fall into: the burden of expectations. Parents, siblings, that cousin who made it big and now everyone compares you to them. It piles up.

I used to see expectations as weight on my shoulders. Now I see them differently. When someone expects something from you, it means they believe you're capable of it. Nobody expects things from people they've given up on. Think about that. The last time you expected something from someone knowing full well they wouldn't deliver, how'd that feel? You don't do it. Expectations are a sign that someone still thinks you can do more.

The scarier scenario isn't people expecting too much from you. It's people expecting nothing.

---

I've made the mistake of pushing back on people who held me to standards I set myself. Friends who got angry when I fell below my own moral bar. My first reaction was defensive. "Who are you to tell me?" Classic ego move. But they weren't being unfair. They were reminding me of something I'd declared, something I believed in, and I was the one walking away from it.

Sometimes the people who love you hold up a mirror, and your first instinct is to break it. That's not their problem. That's yours.

---

So here's what I'm taking away from a 125-year-old poem. Don't try to do it alone. The people who look like they did are lying, or at least the story being told about them is. Believe in something and act on it. Your actions will create belief in others, and that collective belief is what carries you through the 90% of the journey that's just exhaustion and doubt. And stop treating expectations like a burden. They're a signal that someone hasn't given up on you yet.

Kipling wrote "you'll be a man, my son." Maybe that was the test in 1910. For us, I don't think it is. The real test isn't standing alone when everything falls apart. It's letting people stand next to you when you're not sure you deserve it. That's the part my generation forgot how to do.
