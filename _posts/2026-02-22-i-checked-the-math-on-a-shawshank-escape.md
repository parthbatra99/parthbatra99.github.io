It was 2 a.m. and I should've been asleep. Instead, I was lying in bed doing back-of-the-napkin calculations about whether Andy Dufresne could actually dig through that wall in 19 years with a tiny rock hammer. This is what happens when you spend years doing maths and still can't turn off your brain at night.

Turns out, the math checks out beautifully. And that simple fact sent me down a rabbit hole of seven ideas I picked up last month that I haven't been able to stop thinking about.

## Lesson 1: Shawshank math is surprisingly honest

Let me walk you through it. The tunnel looked roughly 10 meters long and half a meter wide based on Andy's shoulder width. Assuming a circular cross-section, that's a volume of about 1.96 cubic meters. With a density of roughly 1,600 kg per cubic meter for concrete and sand, we're talking about 3,136 kg of material to remove.

Nineteen years is 6,935 days. If Andy scraped out between half a liter and one liter per day, he'd remove about 5.2 cubic meters in that time. He only needed 1.96. The guy could've skipped every other day and still made it.

Here's what hit me: the dreams you think will take 600 years are hardly 20 years away. That's a 30x gap. Even if real life isn't a movie and the factor is only 5x or 3x, the thing you're certain will take a decade? It might take three and a half years. My master's advisor said something similar when I started: small extra effort every day compounds brutally over 150 to 200 weeks.

I wrote something on a sticky note a while back that still holds up: tiny steps taken consistently over a long period of time result in magic. The Shawshank math proves it.

## Lesson 2: Stop mixing meetings with creative work

Paul Graham wrote a blog post years ago called ["Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule"](https://www.paulgraham.com/makersschedule.html) and it explained something I'd been feeling but couldn't articulate.

The manager schedule is what we all aspire toward. Back-to-back meetings from 9 to 6. You finish one, walk into the next. It looks productive because important people do it.

The maker schedule is different. You block your entire day for one deep task. Writing code, shooting a video, drafting a research paper. No meetings sprinkled in. No "quick catch-up" at 2 p.m. that destroys your entire afternoon.

I've noticed this firsthand with my writing. If I have a meeting at 2, my brain starts preparing for it at noon. I can't focus after it ends until at least 3:30. By then, the day is gone. That single meeting didn't cost one hour. It cost five.

Now I try to stack all my meetings on one day and protect the other days entirely. My own writing improved noticeably once I started doing this. Everything creative requires a maker schedule. And yes, solving equations counts as creative work.

## Lesson 3: Energy isn't exhaustible

[Graham Weaver](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=P0Zgr9xwu4Y), a professor at Stanford, made a point that sounds corny until you sit with it. Energy, he argues, is not exhaustible. Not the physics kind. The human kind.

Think about it. You kiss someone you love today. Does that make you less capable of kissing them tomorrow? You play with your dog today. Less capable of playing with your dog tomorrow? Of course not.

Burnout doesn't come from doing too much. It comes from friction. That gap between what you want to be doing and what you have to be doing. That gap is what exhausts you.

I'm not naive. We all have responsibilities, families, bills. You can't just quit everything and follow your passion. That's terrible advice. But you can find the thing you love and do it at a smaller scale. I make these videos not at the scale I'd like, but at the scale I can manage alongside a full-time job. And it gives me something to look forward to every single week. As long as it's exciting, I'll keep doing it. When it stops being exciting, I'll switch topics. Don't worry, it'll be hard to get rid of me.

## Lesson 4: Your iPhone is lying to you about progress

I've been reading [*Chip War*](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Chip-War/Chris-Miller/9781982172008) and one idea from it has been stuck in my head. We've been trained to expect linear technological progress. New iPhone every year, 20% faster, 20% more efficient. Science doesn't work that way.

A colleague recently shipped a project that took almost two years. For the first year and a half, nothing worked. Just optimization, failure, more optimization. Then devices started working and results came fast. If they were a smartphone company, they'd have to show progress every single year. How do you show progress when your devices aren't working yet?

Here's what companies actually do. Say a manufacturing breakthrough gives a 35% improvement in processor performance. They don't ship all 35% at once. They split it: 20% this year, 20% next year. The growth came in one big chunk, but they sell it in a line.

Also, those bar charts companies show at keynotes? A 35% improvement bar drawn next to the previous year's bar is sometimes three times taller, not a third taller. That's not 35%. That's 3x. Big companies are straight up manipulating data visualization and nobody calls them out.

Hardware progress has been slowing for 15 to 20 years. We're at the physical limits of how small a transistor can get. The improvements you see now are mostly software, not hardware. They're not the same thing.

## Lesson 5: Become the person who improves others by just being thought of

From [*The Daily Stoic*](https://dailystoic.com/), March 10th:

> "Happy is the person who can improve others not only when present but even in their thoughts."

Read that again. There are people who make us better when they're around. Then there are people who make us better just by existing in our memory. You're about to do something you wouldn't want them to see, and their face pops into your head, and you stop.

It hit me hard because I've felt this. A friend, a parent, a partner. Their very thought makes you choose better. It's not the same as religion's universal watcher concept, where fear of being seen keeps you in line. This is different. This comes from love, not fear. Their love inspires you to be a better human, which feels fundamentally different from being scared into being a good one.

If you haven't felt this yet, it'll come when you find people who truly trust you. Hard to put into words. The philosopher did it better than I can.

## Lesson 6: Make it easy to eat healthy

This one's practical. The best fitness hack I used during my bachelor's was embarrassingly simple: make healthy food easier to reach than junk food.

In my hostel, evening snacks were always fried, oily stuff. Kids love fried stuff. So I kept brown bread, bananas, nuts, and a small portion of whatever snack I was craving in my room. I'd eat the nuts and fruit first, then have a small piece of the savory stuff. The 30 grams of nuts (one handful, roughly 200 calories of healthy fat) would fill me up enough that the craving for junk was mostly gone.

I had a friend whose room was next to mine. We'd buy the same 200-gram snack pack on the same day. Mine would last a month. Two days later, we'd be walking somewhere and he'd say he needed to buy another one. Two days. The bananas and bread in his room sat untouched while the snack vanished.

So the idea doesn't work for everyone. But it worked for me, and it might work for you. The principle is simple: carry something healthy with you. Eat it first. The craving handles itself.

## The thread connecting all seven

I started this whole exercise because I couldn't sleep and a movie bothered me enough to do arithmetic at 2 a.m. But the thread through all these lessons is the same: we wildly misjudge what's possible because we never actually run the numbers.

Andy's 600-year tunnel takes 19. A "quick meeting" costs a full day. The energy you think you've run out of is still there. The processor improvement chart is lying. The person you're thinking of right now is already making you better without knowing it.

Run the math. The gap between what you assume and what's true is usually where the interesting stuff lives.
