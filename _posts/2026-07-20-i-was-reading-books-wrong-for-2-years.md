The year was 2021. I picked up my first book and it took me 3, maybe 4, maybe 5 months to finish it. I was bad at reading. So I went online for advice, like you do. Everyone on the internet was reading 20, 40, even 100 books a year. Naturally, I thought the answer was simple: read faster, read more. I was wrong. It took me 2 full years to figure out why.

The pace at which you read a book should be decided by the *purpose* for which you're reading it. Not by a number some stranger on YouTube hit last December. Not by a Goodreads challenge. By the purpose.

## There are only two reasons to read a book

In my understanding, every book falls into one of two buckets. You're either reading to **gain information**, or you're reading to **change your behavior**. That's it. Once you understand which bucket you're in, the right reading speed becomes obvious. You don't have to force it.

### Bucket one: gain information

These are books where the real value comes *after* you finish reading. You're collecting information so you can apply it somewhere. The outcome is achieved through continuous application, not through the act of reading itself.

I recently read *When a Scientist Presents*, a book about how to give research presentations. Font sizes, figure placement, how to convey results to experts and non-experts alike. Now, I could read that book five times cover to cover and my presentations would still be mediocre if I never actually *made* any. The book gives you the blueprint. The skill comes from building.

Books in this category should be read as fast as you comfortably can. Absorb the information, get out, and start applying.

Another example: *Factfulness* by Hans Rosling. It walks through how the world is actually doing. Child mortality rates, how wealth is distributed, the real dividing lines between people. The biggest insight I got? We are not divided by countries, continents, race, or ethnicity. The biggest divide is wealth and education. Houses built by people with the same income look the same whether you're in India, Africa, or Latin America. Rich people in Delhi live like rich people in Munich. We draw all these arbitrary boundaries, but money and education are the actual ones.

### Where information becomes knowledge

Here's the part most people skip. Reading a book is one-way communication. The author is creating scenarios, asking questions, building arguments. The transformation from information to knowledge happens inside your head, not on the page.

It happens when you stop and *think*. When you imagine yourself in the scenario the author described and figure out how you'd respond. Without that mental exercise, information stays information. And information is free in 2025. There's zero point in paying for a book just to memorize facts you could Google in ten seconds.

### Bucket two: change your behavior

This is the trickier category, but it's also where books give you the most. Nothing here is external. No goal to hit, no metric to optimize. Everything is internal. You're trying to evolve into a better version of yourself, and evolution takes time. Lots of it.

Think of these books like a compass. A compass cannot carry you to your destination. It can only point you in the right direction. You still have to walk.

I started reading *The Daily Stoic* in September of last year. And here's the honest truth: you can read 10, 20, even 100 books on stoicism and it will not make you stoic. The book is a guide. The change comes from you moving in that direction, day after day. These books should be read slowly. Rushing through them gains you nothing.

Then there's *The Almanack of Naval Ravikant*, which is basically Naval's tweets turned into a book. Whenever I'm lost, and I mean properly lost, I open it to a random page. Page 178: "The greatest superpower is the ability to change yourself." I read the explanation, sit with it, think about it. Every time I do this, I somehow end up with exactly the advice I needed. Mere coincidence? Maybe. But it keeps working.

## When you pick the wrong bucket

This is the embarrassing part. I started reading *The Art of Thinking Clearly* by Rolf Dobelli, a book about cognitive biases in decision making. I filed it under "information" and started speed-reading through it. After a week or two, something felt off. The information was so dense, so personal, that it wasn't meant to be applied to the world. It was meant to be applied to my own brain.

I had misclassified the book entirely.

Once I realized it was actually a behavior-change book, I switched gears. I spent the next 6 months reading it slowly, one bite at a time. That reclassification changed everything about how I experienced the book.

## Do I feel guilty about not finishing books?

Honestly? No. I see books the way I see my friend circle. You have friends from school, friends from the gym, friends you party with. Do you feel guilty that you don't go clubbing with your gym buddy? Of course not. Same energy with books.

If a new book or a new idea sparks curiosity, start reading it. If another one pulls you away mid-chapter, go. The key isn't completion. The key is taking whatever knowledge you gained, from the whole book or just three chapters, and actually doing something with it. Knowledge without execution is just expensive storage.

## The real question

For two years I was optimizing the wrong variable. I was asking "how fast can I read?" when I should have been asking "what is this book trying to do to me?" Once I figured that out, I stopped feeling behind. I stopped comparing my shelf count to strangers. Some books deserve a sprint. Others deserve a six-month walk. Knowing which is which is the actual skill.

Your bookshelf isn't a leaderboard. It's a toolkit. And the best readers aren't the fastest ones. They're the ones who know which tool they're holding.
