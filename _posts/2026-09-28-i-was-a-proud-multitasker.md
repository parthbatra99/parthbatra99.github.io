I used to eat lunch with YouTube shorts in one hand and a group chat in the other. Emails got answered during meetings. Podcasts ran in the background while I coded. And I genuinely believed I was getting more done than the people around me.

Then I went down a rabbit hole about how the brain actually works, and it turns out multitasking is a complete fiction. Your brain doesn't do two things at once. It just switches between them really fast, and it's terrible at it.

## The experiment that ruined my self-image

Neuroscientists at UCSF ran a study that's almost comically simple. They put people in a driving simulator and measured how well they drove. Then they added a second task: identify green circles on signs and press a button when you see one. That's it. Press one button when you see one shape.

Every single participant, across every age group, saw their driving performance drop. Person A might be a naturally better driver than Person B. But Person A while looking for green circles is a worse driver than Person A driving solo. Every time. No exceptions.

Your brain doesn't care how smart you are. It has a fixed cognitive budget, and the moment you split it, both tasks suffer.

This is from the book *The Distracted Mind*, by the way. The same book that quotes Einstein, who did not invent the theory of relativity while multitasking at the Swiss patent office. He needed sustained, uninterrupted focus. Which is inconvenient for those of us who like to feel productive while watching Netflix and ordering groceries simultaneously.

The uncomfortable truth is that your brain is both the thing making the errors and the thing judging the errors. It's like a corrupt auditor approving its own expense reports. You think you're doing fine because the measurement tool is also the thing that's broken.

## What I actually do now

I started paying attention to how often I task-switch in a normal hour, and the number was embarrassing. Easily 15 to 20 switches. Phone notification, Slack message, quick email check, back to code, ooh a new tab.

Each switch takes roughly 25 minutes of full refocus, according to research from UC Irvine. That math doesn't work. You can't switch 20 times an hour if each switch costs 25 minutes of recovery.

Gandhi apparently said there's more to life than increasing its speed. I think about that every time I instinctively grab my phone during a quiet moment. The silence isn't the problem. My discomfort with silence is.

## Unforced errors: the badminton lesson

The same source that introduced me to the multitasking myth also made a point about badminton that I can't stop thinking about. In badminton there are two kinds of errors: forced and unforced. Forced errors happen when your opponent puts you under pressure and you have no good option. Unforced errors are when the shuttle lands right in your court, completely in your control, and you still hit it into the net.

Among the top 10 ranked players in the world, the skill gap is tiny. On any given day, the person who wins is the one who makes fewer unforced errors. Flashier shots don't win matches. Raw talent doesn't either. The one who screws up less when nothing is forcing them to, wins.

I've started categorizing my own mistakes this way. Showing up late to a meeting is an unforced error. Missing a deadline I knew about for two weeks is an unforced error. Picking an unnecessary argument over Slack is an unforced error.

Nobody made me do any of those things. They were entirely within my control.

The terrifying part is how quickly these accumulate. Humans are already bad at overcoming first impressions. Once someone decides you're unreliable, every future interaction gets filtered through that lens. You don't get a reset button.

## 20 push-ups until the deathbed

One more idea that clicked into place. A friend once said something I keep repeating to myself: "I don't want to do 1,000 push-ups in a day. I want to do 20 push-ups until my deathbed."

Health isn't a fuel tank you fill once and coast on. Focus works the same way. So does reliability. It's a small daily effort that has to keep happening.

Three days of strength training, four days of mobility and walking. Nothing heroic or viral-Instagram-reel-worthy. Just consistent, boring, unskippable effort.

The irony is that this principle is the exact opposite of multitasking. Doing one thing at a time, consistently, beats doing everything at once, badly. Every experiment confirms it, and top athletes live by it.

Maybe the real insight isn't about time management at all. It's about ego management. Multitasking feels impressive. Single-tasking feels slow.

The person doing one thing at a time isn't falling behind. They're just not making unforced errors they'll have to fix later.
