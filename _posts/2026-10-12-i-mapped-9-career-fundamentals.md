I've been thinking about that number a lot lately. Not because I'm chasing a billion. But because it tells you something important about what actually drives an extraordinary career, and it's almost never what people assume.

I found a framework of 9 fundamentals that clicked for me. Not theoretical stuff. Real patterns you can see playing out in the careers of people like Sundar Pichai, Satya Nadella, and Mark Zuckerberg. Stuff nobody talks about at career day because it's too specific and too honest.

Here they are, in order, because the order matters.

---

## 1. Health

Yes, health. Fundamental number one. Not "learn to code." Not "build your personal brand." Health.

Look at the people running the biggest companies on the planet. They're all fit. That's not a coincidence. You can't run anything extraordinary if your body keeps breaking down on you. And health is not complicated. Five things: exercise, diet, sleep, don't drink, don't smoke. That's the whole list.

Every time I move to a new city or a new institute, the first question I ask is not "where's the library" or "where's the office." It's "where am I going to work out." Outside, inside, at home, in a park, at a gym. I figure that out first and build everything else around it. Everything else is negotiable. That slot is not.

If you think this sounds like lifestyle advice, you're missing the point. This is career infrastructure. You don't get to be Sundar Pichai if you're too exhausted to think clearly at 3pm.

---

## 2. Who You Work With

You're the average of the five people you spend the most time with. You've heard that before. But the part people skip is that this works both ways. Their discipline becomes your discipline. Their bad habits become your bad habits.

This matters most early in your career when your personality is still wet clay. The people around you will shape your defaults: how you respond to stress, whether you follow through, how seriously you take your word. After a decade, those patterns harden. Changing them later is possible but expensive.

Pick your crew like your career depends on it. Because it does.

---

## 3. Geography

Nobody warned me about this one. Where you are physically located has an outsized effect on what becomes possible.

Steve Jobs grew up in Palo Alto during the semiconductor revolution. Silicon Valley was literally being invented around him. Read his biography and then read *Chip War* and you'll see it clearly: his geography was half his advantage. I don't think he builds Apple if he's born anywhere else.

I live in San Sebastian now. It has the most Michelin star restaurants per square meter of any city on earth. The basque cheesecake is always ranked number one or two globally. Walk down any street and you're surrounded by world class food. The students I meet who are studying gastronomy here are insanely passionate, not because they chose to be, but because the city won't let them be anything else.

Geography isn't entirely in your control. You don't pick where you're born. But when small opportunities come up to relocate somewhere with a better ecosystem for what you do, take them. Don't overthink it.

---

## 4. What You Work On

Here's where it gets tricky, because whatever you pick might be obsolete in 3 years. AI is already rewriting job descriptions weekly.

The key insight: decide what you work on based on who you are, not what's trending.

We used to live in a "one thing" economy. You were a doctor. A lawyer. A teacher. That was your whole identity. Einstein couldn't have taught students in India from Switzerland. Now? You can be a researcher and a teacher. A doctor and a painter. Someone who codes by day and makes music by night. I'm a researcher who shares what I learn publicly. That combination didn't exist 30 years ago.

The world is connected enough now that you can design a career around your actual self instead of squeezing yourself into a job title. Use that.

---

## 5. Specific Knowledge

This one comes from Naval Ravikant. Specific knowledge is the thing you're naturally good at, or the thing you can get really good at with unusually low effort. It can't be taught in school, because if it could, someone would teach it to someone cheaper and replace you.

How do you find it? Look at your childhood. Look at the environment you grew up in. What skills did you pick up without trying? What came easy when everything else felt like pushing a boulder uphill?

Here's the twist. Your disadvantages are the raw material. Viktor Axelsen, arguably the greatest badminton player alive right now, said something that's been rattling around my head: "Nothing is so bad that it's not good for something else." Every disadvantage you carry has a hidden advantage baked into it. That's not motivational poster nonsense. It's how 85% of billionaires happened.

If being born rich was the real advantage, why do so many extraordinary artists, musicians, and founders come from nothing? Because comfort kills creativity. When your brain never had to figure out unconventional solutions to real problems, it never learned how.

---

## 6. What You Do After 5 PM

Your skill gets you the job. What you do with your evenings determines what job you have in 3 years.

I run experiments. Pick one thing, give it 6 months. If I'm making progress, I stick with it. If not, I try something else. After 2 or 3 cycles of this, you end up with something genuinely interesting. Either it supercharges your primary career or it becomes your new career entirely. Both outcomes are fine.

No job is truly full time. You always have more free time than you think. The question is whether you're using it or filling it.

---

## 7. Action Bias

This one I picked up from *Million Dollar Weekend* by Noah Kagan, and it's deceptively powerful. Action bias is your ability to break inertia and take the first step.

Think about exercise again. The hardest part of any workout isn't the last rep. It's the first one. The moment you start, you'll finish. Your body wants to maintain whatever state it's in. If it's sitting, it wants to keep sitting. If it's moving, it wants to keep moving. Newton figured this out 340 years ago and it applies to your career just as much as it applies to physics.

You can train this muscle. My rule: any task that takes less than 2 minutes gets done immediately. Not in my notes. Not on a list. Right now. Rent payment? Done. Dirty cup in the sink? Washed. The action muscle in your brain gets stronger every time you use it, until one day you're making bigger decisions without flinching because the default has shifted from "I'll think about it" to "let's go."

---

## 8. Nonlinear Skills

Some skills give you disproportionate returns. English is the obvious one. It's the working language of the planet. If you can speak it, you can find work almost anywhere.

But the real magic is the second layer. Once you speak English, learn another language. German. Spanish. Mandarin. Whatever clicks. Will it help your job directly? Maybe, maybe not. But it unlocks movies, poetry, books, and ideas you literally cannot access otherwise. And at the end of the day, you only need one idea to change your life.

Nonlinear skills are the ones where the input and output don't match up in a neat line. Small investment, absurd payoff. Language. Writing. Public speaking. The ability to tell a story. These don't show up in job descriptions but they show up in every promotion.

---

## 9. Switch

Not switch jobs. Switch yourself.

When computers arrived, people lost jobs. When AI arrived, people lost jobs. But the people who lost jobs weren't the ones whose skills became obsolete. They were the ones who refused to adapt. The ones who dug in and said "this is how I've always done it."

New technology destroys old jobs and creates new ones. YouTube didn't exist before the internet. Web development didn't exist before browsers. Blogging didn't exist before platforms. The only people who got left behind were the ones who wouldn't change.

A healthy body is a flexible body. A healthy mind is a flexible mind. The people who build extraordinary careers aren't the ones who picked the right thing once. They're the ones who kept picking the right thing every time the ground shifted under them.

---

Most people treat their career like a slot machine: pull the lever, hope for the best. But the ones who build something extraordinary aren't luckier. They just stopped pulling the lever and started building the machine.
