Here's a stat that will mess with you: 41.6 million Americans went to college but never finished. Dropouts, all of them. On the entire planet, there are 3,028 billionaires. Yet somehow we all "know" that dropping out is the billionaire move.

Steve Jobs did it. Zuckerberg did it. Gates did it. So did 41,599,997 other people you've never heard of, because nobody writes a magazine profile about the guy who dropped out and makes $22,000 a year.

I spent 107 days going deep on cognitive biases. The first thing I learned is that my brain has been lying to me with confidence my entire life. Not occasionally. Constantly.

The good news is there's a pattern to the lies, and once you see them, you can't unsee them.

---

## Bias #1: Survivorship bias, only counting the winners

You look at billionaires who dropped out and your brain connects the dots. "Dropping out = success." Clean narrative, satisfying conclusion, completely wrong.

75% of billionaires have a college degree. The dropout billionaires are the exception, not the rule. But you don't hear about the 41.6 million who dropped out and are earning less than their graduating classmates.

That's not a fun story. Nobody retweets it.

Think about it from a media perspective. Someone drops out of IIT, starts a company, and makes a billion dollars. Front page news.

That same person drops out and makes less than their peers? Not a story. You literally took yourself out of a golden opportunity and came out behind. Who's writing that headline?

The media doesn't report reality. It reports interesting deviations from reality. And your brain treats those deviations as the norm.

I caught myself doing this with startup founders. I'd read about someone who quit their FAANG job, built a SaaS, and hit $1M ARR in 18 months. My brain immediately said "that could be me." What it didn't say: for every one of those stories, there are thousands who quit, burned through savings, and quietly went back to job hunting.

I just never met them because they don't post about it on LinkedIn.

## Bias #2: Sunk cost fallacy, throwing good money after bad

You buy a movie ticket for $20. Fifteen minutes in, you know it's garbage. Do you leave?

Of course not. You sit there for another two and a half hours because you already paid. Here's the math your brain refuses to do: that $20 is gone whether you stay or go. By staying, you're investing additional time.

If your hourly rate is $20, you just spent $50 more to "recover" $20. A bad movie that should have cost you $20 now costs you $70, and $50 of that was voluntary.

If this were just about movies, who cares. But the same pattern plays out everywhere.

You invest $100 in a stock. It drops to $90. Instead of pulling out and putting that $90 somewhere that returns 50% (which would give you $135, a 35% gain on your original investment), you hold.

You hold because selling means admitting the loss. So the stock keeps falling and you keep holding, waiting to "break even" on a decision that was already wrong.

The most dangerous version? Relationships. You can't put a number on emotions, which makes sunk cost fallacy almost impossible to spot in real time. You've invested six months, a year, three years. You know it's not going anywhere.

But leaving means all that time was "wasted," so you invest another year. And another. The math is identical to the movie ticket.

The cost is already sunk. The only question is whether you keep paying.

## Bias #3: Confirmation bias, seeing what you already believe

Plot number of babies against family wealth for Christians. As wealth goes up, babies go down. Plot it for Hindus, Buddhists, Sikhs. Same curve every time.

Now plot it for Muslims. Identical.

We are not divided by religion on this one. We're divided by wealth.

But if you grew up in a neighborhood where a particular religious community was economically weaker and had more kids, your brain didn't plot the data. It saw a pattern, skipped the confounding variable, and filed it under "how the world works." Now every time you see data that confirms it, you nod. Every time you see data that contradicts it, you find a reason to dismiss it.

This is confirmation bias, and it's the sneakiest one because it feels like you're just being observant.

I read a line in *Factfulness* that stuck with me. The authors describe couples across the United States, Iran, Mexico, Malaysia, Brazil, Italy, China, Indonesia, India, Colombia, Bangladesh, South Africa, Libya, and they're all doing the same thing in their bedrooms. Whispering about their dreams for their kids' future. Different languages, different scriptures on the nightstand, same whispered hope.

Every language has a word for "happy." Every language has a word for "grief." That's not a coincidence. It's evidence that humans are running on nearly identical emotional hardware, and the things we think divide us are usually just the things we haven't looked at closely enough.

---

## So what do you do with this?

Every time you hear a compelling story about someone's success, ask: how many people tried the same thing and failed? Every time you're holding onto something because you've "already invested so much," ask: if I were starting fresh today, would I make the same choice? Every time a belief feels obviously true, ask: what data would change my mind?

If you can't answer that last question, you're not thinking. You're just confirming.
