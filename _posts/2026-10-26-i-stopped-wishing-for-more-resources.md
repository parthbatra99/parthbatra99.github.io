Name one great songwriter who was born a billionaire.

I'll wait.

The pattern is so consistent it's almost a joke: the people who create the most extraordinary things are rarely the ones who started with the most resources. There's something about having less that forces a kind of thinking that abundance just... doesn't.

I've been thinking about this a lot lately, and the more I look at it, the more convinced I am that disadvantages aren't detours. They're the actual route.

---

Aristotle said it 2,300 years ago and nobody listened: "We are what we do. Excellence is not an act, it's a habit." Which sounds like a motivational poster until you actually sit with it. Excellence isn't a moment of brilliance. It's the thing you do on Tuesday when nobody's watching.

Every time I see an extraordinary athlete on screen, I ask myself the same question. What's the actual difference between them and me? The hardware is more or less the same. Limbs intact, all the standard equipment. If you could download Cristiano Ronaldo's brain into my head, how big would the gap be?

My bet: not orders of magnitude. Probably not even 2x. The hardware is shockingly similar across humans. The difference is the software. The accumulated habits, the thousands of small decisions that compound into something that looks like talent from the outside.

---

Here's where it gets interesting. All the disadvantages we carry? They're loading specific software.

If you grew up without much money, you had to figure things out. You couldn't throw money at problems. You had to be creative by default, not by choice. And that constraint-based creativity compounds the same way habits do.

Have you ever noticed that the best music, the most original art, the wildest solutions come from people who had to scrape for everything? That's not romanticization. That's mechanics. When resources are scarce, your brain stops looking for the expensive option and starts looking for the *right* option. Necessity doesn't just invent the mother of invention. It is the invention.

---

I caught myself doing the same thing with languages recently, and it blew open a whole other realization.

In Hindi, there's *bhai*, meaning brother. And then there's *bhaiya*, also brother, but polite. A formal register for your own sibling. English has nothing like this. No "brother" and "polite brother." Neither does English have an equivalent for *karm*. We translate it as "work," but that's like translating "ocean" as "water." Technically accurate, spiritually bankrupt.

The reason these words exist in Hindi and not in English isn't random. Languages originate from need. If a society consistently needs to express something, eventually a word crystallizes around it. If Hindi has a polite form of brother, it means there was enough social hierarchy and family structure that people *regularly needed* to distinguish between casual brother and respectful brother. The word is an archaeological artifact of the culture.

---

This works in reverse too. In countries like Germany and Sweden, higher education costs roughly nothing. Healthcare is handled. The social safety net catches you before you hit the ground. So there's never a situation where parents need to drain their life savings for a child's education, or where a medical emergency wipes out a family's future.

The sacrifice Indian parents make (and I can speak for mine) is a specific kind of sacrifice born from a specific kind of absence. Swedish parents love their kids just as much. They just don't need to express that love through financial devastation. Different conditions, different words, different habits.

I think in English for about 80% of my waking hours now. Work, research, writing. It's all English. But when I switch to Hindi, I'm a different person. Not a translated version of the same person. A genuinely different thinking pattern. Your brain physically moves differently depending on which language it's running.

---

Constraints breeding creativity. Languages encoding society. Habits compounding into excellence. All of it points to the same thing. Your conditions don't just affect what you do. They affect what you *become*.

And here's where I realized I'd been approaching my own work completely backwards.

For years, my hierarchy was: hard work first, efficiency second, effectiveness last. Grind harder. Then find a smarter way. Then, maybe, ask if the thing was worth doing at all.

That's the exact wrong order.

Effectiveness means asking: *Can I eliminate this task entirely?* If yes, stop. No efficiency needed, no hard work required. You just saved yourself 100% of the effort.

If you can't eliminate it, then ask: *What's the most efficient way to do this?* Optimize the path.

Only after you've eliminated what you can and optimized what remains do you put in the hard work. Because now the hard work is directed. It's not effort spent on something that shouldn't exist in the first place.

I was stupid to follow it the other way around. But I think most people do.

---

There's a quote that goes: "The factories of the future will have only two employees, a man and a dog. The man is there to feed the dog. The dog is there to keep the man away from the equipment."

It sounded like a joke when it was coined. It sounds like a project timeline now.

As AI accelerates, the people who thrive won't be the ones who can work the hardest. They'll be the ones who can figure out what to eliminate, what to optimize, and where the actual human effort still matters. The constraints aren't going away. They're just shifting from "I don't have money" to "I don't have relevance."

Same engine. Different fuel.

---

The people with the fewest resources have always been the most creative ones. Not because suffering is noble. Because constraints force you to ask better questions. And better questions are the one thing that no amount of money can buy.
